Electrical Resistivity / VES in Cheyenne: Mapping Subsurface Layers Without Excavation

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

LEARN MORE →

Cheyenne sits at 6,062 feet above sea level, the highest state capital in the country. That elevation comes with a unique subsurface reality: shallow bedrock, expansive clays, and wide variations in groundwater depth across the metro area. When a civil project needs to map those layers without turning the site into a grid of boreholes, electrical resistivity becomes the go-to tool. We run VES surveys across Laramie County to profile soil and rock interfaces, detect water-bearing zones, and identify buried channels that standard drilling can miss. The method sends a controlled current into the ground through four electrodes, measuring how the earth resists it. Different materials respond differently, and that difference tells the story of what lies beneath. For projects near Crow Creek or out by the Warren AFB expansion zones, a CPT test can later verify the resistivity contacts with direct penetration data, but the geophysical picture comes first.

A single VES sounding can map three to five geoelectric layers down to 100 feet in under two hours, without disturbing the surface.

Our approach and scope

The subsurface contrast between northwest Cheyenne and the areas south of I-80 is exactly why we rely on electrical methods before committing to a drilling program. Up near the Frontier Park sandstone outcrops, resistivity values often spike above 200 ohm-m within the first 20 feet, signaling competent bedrock close to grade. South of the interstate, where Quaternary alluvium fills paleochannels, the same depth can read 15 to 40 ohm-m, typical of saturated silts and fine sands. Vertical Electrical Sounding captures this variability in a single sounding curve that we invert with 1D or 2D software to produce a true resistivity cross-section. We use Schlumberger arrays for deep penetration and Wenner arrays when lateral resolution matters more. Every sounding follows ASTM D6431 guidelines, with electrode spacings calibrated to the target depth. The data feeds directly into foundation design decisions, dewatering plans, and even contamination plume mapping for environmental compliance work across Cheyenne's industrial corridors.

Local ground factors

A four-story mixed-use building off Lincolnway hit a buried clay channel at 12 feet that the preliminary geotechnical borings had missed entirely. The clay was saturated, electrically conductive, and compressible. When we ran a VES line across the lot, the resistivity profile showed the channel clearly: a 15-foot-deep lens of 8 ohm-m material sandwiched between 60 ohm-m sandy gravels. The structural engineer had designed spread footings on the assumption of uniform bearing strata, and that assumption was wrong. We had to redesign the foundation with deeper piers at the channel crossing. Cost: about $40,000 in change orders. Time lost: three weeks. A $900 resistivity survey before the borings would have caught the anomaly on day one. Cheyenne's geology is full of these surprises. The Pierre Shale weathers unevenly, the alluvium shifts across short distances, and the groundwater table fluctuates seasonally by several feet. Electrical methods reveal the patterns that single-point borings cannot.

Quick answers

How deep can a VES survey reach in Cheyenne's geology?

With Schlumberger arrays and expanded electrode spacing, we routinely reach 80 to 100 feet in Cheyenne's sedimentary formations. The practical limit depends on the site: high-resistivity sandstone near the surface can reduce current penetration, while saturated clays allow deeper investigation. For targets deeper than 100 feet, we combine VES with seismic methods.

What does a VES survey cost for a typical residential or commercial lot in Cheyenne?

A standard VES survey on a single lot in Cheyenne runs between US$720 and US$980, depending on target depth, array configuration, and number of soundings. Larger commercial parcels with multiple sounding points are quoted per linear foot of survey line after a site walk.

How do you calibrate resistivity data to actual soil types?

Resistivity is an indirect measurement, so we calibrate every survey against at least one borehole or test pit log. When a known lithology at a known depth produces a specific resistivity value, we use that as a reference to interpret the rest of the profile. This calibration step is required under ASTM D6431 and eliminates ambiguity in the geoelectric interpretation.

Can VES surveys be performed on paved or urban sites in Cheyenne?

Yes, but with modifications. On asphalt or concrete, we use bentonite mud or conductive gel couplants at each electrode stake to maintain low contact resistance. Urban sites with buried utilities require a pre-survey utility locate. We avoid electrode lines parallel to buried metallic pipes, which can distort the current field and produce misleading low-resistivity anomalies.
